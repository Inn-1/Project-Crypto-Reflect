Fig. 01: The problem

What the loop is, and where it happens

An Arctic feedback loop is a cycle where warming causes changes that lead to even more warming.

It begins with reflection. Snow and ice are bright, so they send most of the sunlight that hits them back out to space. As they melt, they uncover the much darker land and ocean underneath, and those darker surfaces absorb the sunlight as heat instead. That heat melts more ice, which uncovers more dark surface, which absorbs more heat. Each turn of the cycle makes the next one a little easier.

Melting ice is not the only cause. Thawing permafrost, extra water vapour in the air, and the release of greenhouse gases all add heat to the same loop. The problem is happening at three scales at once. Select a marker on the map to explore.

Ecosystems

Melting ice destroys habitat for polar bears and seals and removes the ice algae that the whole marine food web is built on. Thawing permafrost also releases methane, which traps far more heat than carbon dioxide in the short term.

People

For Arctic Indigenous communities, losing stable ice threatens hunting and travel routes that have been used for generations. Further away, the meltwater raises sea levels, and the people most at risk often did the least to cause the warming.

Technology

Geoengineering ideas like Project Cryo-Reflect could help, but they are hard to build at scale. There is also a risk that a promising technology becomes an excuse to stop cutting emissions, which is the real problem.

Fig. 02: The mechanism

The ice-albedo loop, step by step

Albedo is just a measure of how reflective a surface is. Sea ice has high albedo and works like a mirror. Dark ocean water has low albedo and behaves more like a sponge for heat.

The reason this loop is so dangerous is that nothing in it pushes back. Once the melting starts, every step makes the next step happen more easily. The same pattern shows up in the permafrost-carbon loop, where warming wakes up soil microbes that release carbon, which causes more warming. To slow either loop, you have to interrupt one of its steps before it feeds the next one.

Fig. 03: Three sciences

One problem, read three ways

No single subject explains the Arctic on its own. Biology shows how the damage spreads through living things, chemistry explains why the gases trap heat, and physics explains the light and energy that start the whole process and gives us the tools to watch it.

BiologyEcosystems

Damage that climbs the food web

Ice algae grow under the sea ice and feed almost everything in the Arctic ocean. As the ice retreats they lose their home, and the base of the food web thins out. Warming also shifts the timing of the spring bloom, so zooplankton hatch and find their food already gone. Scientists call this a trophic mismatch.

ice algae → zooplankton → Arctic cod → seals → polar bears

The strain reaches all the way to the top. Female polar bears that cannot store enough fat struggle to carry a pregnancy. The biological responses work with the system rather than against it: bringing back grazing herds, protecting peatlands, and conserving the marine life that stores carbon and feeds the food web at the same time.

ChemistryReactions

Why the gases hold heat

Burning fossil fuels and thawing permafrost both add greenhouse gases to the air. Carbon dioxide and water vapour absorb the heat radiating off Earth and send it back in every direction, which acts like a blanket around the planet.

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + energy

Methane is the bigger danger in the short term, because it traps much more heat than carbon dioxide before it breaks down after about ten to twelve years. The chemical solutions are carbon capture, which uses amine liquids to trap carbon dioxide at the source, and hydrogen fuel cells, which make electricity and leave only water behind.

PhysicsLight & optics

Measuring and bending the light

Optics is how we keep watch on the loop. Satellites like the European Space Agency's Sentinel-3 track how the Arctic's reflectivity is changing, and the GRACE-FO mission uses lasers to measure the Greenland Ice Sheet losing mass.

Optics also offers a way to act. Spreading reflective silica over thin ice raises its albedo, and on land, solar thermal collectors, solar panels, and a renewable grid cut the emissions that drive the warming in the first place.

Fig. 04: The action plan

Two actions, one for each loop

The plan works on both feedback loops at the same time. One action restores the Arctic's reflectivity, and the other keeps its buried carbon frozen. Neither one replaces cutting emissions. They are meant to slow things down while that harder work goes on.

Action 01: Ice-albedo loop

Project Cryo-Reflect

Spread a thin layer of tiny hollow glass beads, made from silica, across young and thin sea ice. The beads reflect sunlight back toward space before the dark water beneath can absorb it. In effect, they replace some of the reflective surface the Arctic is losing.

Why it works

Fresh sea ice reflects up to 85 percent of the sunlight that reaches it, while open water absorbs most of it. Raising the reflectivity of the thinnest, most vulnerable ice slows the melt before it can speed itself up.

What we expect

Treated areas should hold their ice longer through summer and give it a chance to thicken, with satellites measuring the difference. It is a regional measure, not a cure.

Action 02: Permafrost-carbon loop

Keeping the ground frozen

Use living systems to protect the permafrost. Large grazing animals like reindeer and musk oxen pack down the winter snow, which lets the cold reach the soil. Protecting and rewetting peatlands keeps their soil waterlogged so the carbon inside stays locked away.

Why it works

Deep snow acts like a blanket and keeps the ground warmer than the air. Trampling it removes that blanket. Waterlogged peat has almost no oxygen, so the dead plants in it never fully break down. Both methods stop carbon from escaping in the first place.

What we expect

Carbon and methane stay in the ground, and the tundra and peatland stay healthy. Once it is set up, this approach mostly looks after itself.

0-2 yearsShort term

Run small, carefully monitored bead trials with full environmental safety testing, and protect the peatlands that are still intact before they can be drained.

2-5 yearsMedium term

If the trials are safe and they work, expand them with automated systems, grow the herbivore projects, and start rewetting drained peatlands, comparing treated areas with untreated ones by satellite.

5+ yearsLong term

Tie everything to a shift toward renewable energy that cuts emissions and black carbon, so reflectivity, permafrost, and clean power all support each other.

What it needs

Funding for manufacturing, deployment, and satellite monitoring. Engineers, ecologists, and climate scientists working together. Land access and long-term monitoring for the biological side.

Who is involved

Arctic Indigenous communities are partners from the start. Their knowledge of local ice and land guides where to act, and as the people most affected, they have a real say in anything placed in their region.

Fig. 05: Reflection

What we took away

The biggest thing we learned is that no single subject could explain this problem by itself, and that the most honest plans are the ones that admit what they cannot do.

Bringing the subjects together

Physics explained why the ice reflects sunlight and why losing it matters so much. Chemistry explained why methane and carbon dioxide trap heat. Biology showed how the damage moves through the food web, from ice algae up to polar bears. The picture only made sense once we put all three side by side.

What was difficult

A lot of the science is still uncertain. The silica beads are experimental, and no one fully knows yet whether they could harm marine life, so it was hard to write about them with much confidence. It was also tempting to treat one idea as the whole answer, which it is not.

What we realised

The solutions that make the most sense work with the Arctic's natural systems instead of fighting them. We also saw how easily a new technology can become a reason to stop cutting emissions, when cutting emissions is still the most important thing we can do.

What we would change

We would put emissions reduction at the centre of the plan and treat the other actions as ways to slow the loop down while that happens. We would also want a lot more testing before using any of these methods at a large scale.

The loop is self-reinforcing. It is not unbreakable.

Every step that gets interrupted, whether by reflecting light, freezing carbon, or cutting emissions, slows the cycle down and gives the rest of the planet more time.
